Godot Or Go Home! - July 31, 2018

The Little Game Engine That Could

So I've been slowly plugging away at Udacity's Android Developer course series, and although I'm enjoying it, I'm not getting as much satisfaction as I was hoping to. I keep having visions of this grand video game that I've been wanting to build for awhile. I've even started working on it a bit! Game design is something I keep coming back to because it encompasses all three of my main passions: Art, Music and Coding. Put it all together and it is one of the best ways I can express myself. I've practiced with Unity quite a bit over the past couple years, and I wouldn't say I'm professional or anything, but I can make simple things. But if I want to truly make a name for myself, I don't think Unity is the right engine for that. Enter: Godot (pronounced gah-doh). It's a super lightweight game engine that can keep up with some of the heavy hitters.

Open Source

One of my favorite things about Godot is that it is entirely open source. There is a strong developer community behind it, constantly improving it and making it a better contender with industry standards. It is licensed under the MIT license, which means that anything I make with it is completely my own. Any profits from a game I create go directly to me and my team should I choose to work with one. No need to pay for licensing or use the engine's splash screen at the begginning of the game (although I totally will with Godot becuase I love to support open source projects). I can also download the source code for the engine and modify it to my heart's content without worry of legal ramifications.

Excellent Workflow

The layout of the Godot editor is something I really like as well. Everything about Godot speaks efficiency. From it's miniscule download size (about 40 mb) to its own scripting language. This engine is built for speed. I built the first little tutorial game in the documentation in a couple of hours. Although I will have to learn a new scripting language, GDScript isn't too much different than python, so it shouldn't be too difficult.

Less Saturated Market

One huge reason why I'm particularly interested in focusing on the Godot engine is the lack of learning materials. It's still a relatively newer engine compared to some of the others so that leaves a lot of room for newcomers to establish an authority on tutorials and demos. As I'm learning I can grow my Youtube channel into an excellent source for others to learn from. Already I've struggled so much trying to get some things done, I know it would be super beneficial to the Godot community. If I can eventually make a course series or something, it could be an excellent source of income, aside from games, if the value I provide is top notch.

Who Doesn't Love Video Games?

I keep thinking to myself, there must be a reason why I keep coming back to video games. They are something I thoroughly enjoy playing and making them gives me instant gratification. Seeing the art I drew, music I composed and the code I wrote come alive is a feeling like no other, and the hurdles to jump over are challenging enough to keep me interested. So if there's anything that I would love to spend my time on, it would be making video games for people. I really hope one day I can make something and someone, somewhere will say "This is my favorite game!"
